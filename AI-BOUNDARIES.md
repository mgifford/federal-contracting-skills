# AI Boundaries for Federal Contracting Tools

This document defines what 1102tools will not build and why. These boundaries are intentional design decisions based on how the Federal Acquisition Regulation assigns authority, where professional judgment is legally required, and where AI-generated output creates institutional risk.

The tools in this collection automate data retrieval and document structure. They pull from live federal APIs, normalize data across systems, and assemble structured outputs that professionals review and sign. They do not make determinations, score proposals, evaluate performance, or recommend decisions. That line is the architecture of every skill in this collection, and this document explains where the line sits and what happens when you cross it.

---

## The Design Principle

Every skill in this collection follows one rule: **assemble data, structure the document, leave the judgment to the human.**

An IGCE Builder pulls BLS wages, validates against GSA CALC+ ceiling rates, adds per diem travel costs, and produces a multi-sheet workbook. The contracting officer reviews the estimate, adjusts the assumptions, and signs it. The tool drafted. The CO decided.

A Market Research Builder pulls five years of USASpending data, analyzes vendor landscape and competition patterns, and produces a FAR Part 10 report. The CO reviews the analysis, adjusts the set-aside recommendation based on knowledge of the local vendor community, and includes it in the contract file. The tool assembled. The CO determined.

This pattern works because the output is verifiable. A CO can check the BLS wage data. A COR can verify the USASpending award history. The professional applies expertise to data they can see and challenge. When a tool crosses from data assembly into judgment, the output is no longer verifiable -- it is an opinion generated by a system that cannot be cross-examined, does not hold a warrant, and is not accountable for the consequences.

## What These Tools Will Not Do

### Best Value Tradeoff Analysis

Best value tradeoff is the process of comparing technical merit against price across competing proposals to determine which offeror provides the greatest value to the government. This is the contracting officer's warrant authority under FAR 15.101-1. It requires reading proposals, understanding technical approaches, weighing strengths and weaknesses against evaluation criteria, and making a judgment call that balances cost against capability.

An AI cannot do this. It has not read the proposals (they are source selection sensitive). It does not understand the mission context. It cannot weigh a technical strength against a price premium in the way a human evaluator does after days of panel deliberation. An AI-generated best value determination would be a protestable action under GAO's bid protest jurisdiction because the determination was not made by the source selection authority.

Do not build tools that compare proposals against each other. Do not build tools that rank offerors. Do not build tools that recommend award decisions.

### Proposal Evaluation Scoring

Proposal evaluation is governed by FAR 15.305. Evaluators assess proposals against stated evaluation criteria and assign ratings (Outstanding, Good, Acceptable, Marginal, Unacceptable or equivalent). These ratings are the foundation of the source selection decision and the primary subject of GAO bid protests.

An AI scoring proposals against evaluation criteria would be: (1) using source selection information that cannot be shared with external systems, (2) applying judgment that FAR assigns to qualified evaluation panel members, and (3) generating ratings that the source selection authority would need to independently validate, defeating the purpose.

GAO sustains protests when agencies fail to properly evaluate proposals. An AI-generated evaluation would not survive protest scrutiny because the evaluator cannot explain its reasoning, cannot be questioned by the SSA, and cannot participate in consensus discussions.

Do not build tools that score, rate, or evaluate proposals. Do not build tools that generate strengths, weaknesses, or deficiency findings. Do not build tools that assess technical approaches.

### Responsibility Determination Decision Maker

This is distinct from a tool that assembles data for a responsibility review (SAM.gov registration, exclusion status, past award history, FAR 9.104-1 standards). Data assembly is appropriate. The distinction is between a tool that says "here is the entity's registration status, exclusion record, and award history" versus a tool that says "this entity is responsible" or "this entity is not responsible."

The responsibility determination is a contracting officer decision under FAR 9.104-1 that considers financial resources, ability to meet delivery schedules, performance record, integrity, and other factors that often require information beyond what public APIs provide (banking references, facility assessments, conversations with prior CORs). A non-responsibility determination triggers SBA Certificate of Competency procedures for small businesses and has immediate contractual consequences.

Do not build tools that make responsibility determinations. Build tools that assemble the data a CO uses to make that determination.

### CPARS and Past Performance Rating

The Contractor Performance Assessment Reporting System (CPARS) is a controlled-access system. Past performance evaluations contain assessment narratives, ratings, and contractor responses that are source selection sensitive. This data is not available through any public API.

Even if the data were available, generating past performance ratings requires professional judgment about contractor performance in specific contexts. A contractor that delivered late on one contract may have done so because the government changed requirements mid-performance. An AI reading a data feed cannot make that distinction. An AI-generated past performance rating would be challenged in every claim and dispute because the "evaluator" cannot testify to the basis of its assessment.

Do not build tools that rate contractor performance. Do not build tools that generate past performance narratives. Build tools that identify which contracts to evaluate (from USASpending) and structure the evaluation framework (from FAR 15.305(a)(2)), then leave every assessment field for the human evaluator.

### Protest Risk Prediction

A tool that predicts the likelihood of a bid protest based on acquisition characteristics (sole source, incumbent loss, small business set-aside decisions) would create perverse incentives. Contracting officers might avoid legitimate sole-source awards because the AI flagged them as "high protest risk." Agencies might structure competitions to minimize a protest risk score rather than to achieve the best outcome for the government.

Historical protest data is not systematically available via API. GAO protest decisions are published but not in a structured format suitable for statistical modeling. The factors that drive protests are often non-public (disappointed offerors' internal business decisions, legal counsel advice, competitive dynamics between specific vendors).

Do not build tools that predict protest likelihood. Do not build tools that recommend acquisition strategies based on protest avoidance.

### Price Negotiation Strategy

Negotiation is the most human element of contracting. It involves understanding leverage, reading counterpart reactions, making real-time concessions, and reaching agreements that both parties can execute. A contracting officer negotiating a major contract is weighing technical risk, schedule pressure, competitive alternatives, budget constraints, and organizational priorities simultaneously.

An AI negotiation advisor would be dangerous in two ways: for experienced COs, it would be insulting (they know their leverage better than a model trained on general data); for inexperienced COs, it would be a crutch that replaces the development of negotiation skills with algorithmic recommendations that may not account for the specific dynamics at the table.

Do not build tools that recommend negotiation positions, target prices, or concession strategies. Build tools that provide market data (BLS wages, CALC+ rates, comparable awards) that COs use to prepare for negotiations on their own terms.

### Organizational Conflict of Interest Determiner

OCI analysis under FAR 9.5 requires legal interpretation, fact-specific inquiry, and often involves proprietary or classified information about corporate relationships. The three types of OCI (unequal access to information, biased ground rules, impaired objectivity) each require different analysis frameworks and different mitigation strategies.

An AI that says "this is not an OCI" based on public corporate data could miss subsidiary relationships, teaming arrangements, key personnel conflicts, or access to competitively sensitive information that only a thorough investigation would reveal. An AI that says "this is an OCI" could block a legitimate competitive arrangement based on superficial pattern matching.

Do not build tools that determine whether an OCI exists. Do not build tools that recommend OCI mitigation plans. Build tools that identify corporate relationships worth investigating (from SAM.gov entity data and USASpending award history), then leave the legal analysis to the CO and agency counsel.

### Debarment and Suspension Recommender

Debarment and suspension are quasi-judicial actions under FAR 9.4 with due process requirements including notice, opportunity to respond, and a decision by the agency's suspending and debarring official (SDO). These actions affect a contractor's ability to receive any federal contract or assistance and carry serious reputational and financial consequences.

An AI recommending debarment based on public data patterns (adverse event flags, modification patterns, shared addresses) would be reckless. The patterns that suggest fraud in public data may have legitimate explanations that only a formal investigation would reveal. A recommendation to debar based on algorithmic pattern matching, without investigation, violates the procedural protections that exist for good reason.

Do not build tools that recommend debarment, suspension, or referral to the SDO. Build tools that identify patterns worth investigating (entity networks, modification anomalies, competition irregularities), clearly labeled as indicators for further inquiry, not conclusions.

### Contract Type Recommender

Contract type selection under FAR 16.1 is a professional judgment call. FAR 16.104 lists the factors a CO must consider: price competition, price analysis, type and complexity of the requirement, urgency, period of performance, and contractor risk. These factors are inherently subjective and context-dependent.

A tool that says "use FFP" when a CO knows T&M is more appropriate for the requirement undermines the CO's expertise. A tool that says "use cost-reimbursement" for a requirement that could be priced as firm-fixed-price shifts risk to the government unnecessarily.

Do not build tools that recommend contract types. Build tools that present the FAR 16.104 factors and let the CO weight them. Frame as "factor analysis" not "recommendation." Never output "you should use X."

### Set-Aside Decision Engine

Set-aside decisions under FAR 19 are among the most politically sensitive determinations in federal contracting. Small business advocates, large business advocates, socioeconomic program offices, and industry associations all have strong views on how the Rule of Two (FAR 19.502-2) should be applied.

A standalone tool that recommends set-aside type invites scrutiny from every direction. The Market Research Builder already includes set-aside analysis within the full context of a FAR Part 10 report, where it is one finding among many. Extracting that analysis into a standalone decision tool puts a spotlight on a politically charged determination without the surrounding context that justifies it.

Do not build standalone tools that recommend set-aside types. Keep set-aside analysis embedded within market research where it has full context and is presented as one element of a broader assessment.

## The Line

The boundary across every capability in this collection is the same:

**Build tools that retrieve, normalize, and structure data into formats professionals already use. Do not build tools that make the professional's decision.**

A tool that says "here is the data" is an accelerator. A tool that says "here is what you should do" is a replacement. The first makes the professional faster. The second makes the professional unnecessary. The 1102 series -- contracting officers, contract specialists, price analysts, and procurement professionals across every federal agency -- will adopt the first and reject the second. Not because they fear automation, but because they understand that the decisions these tools would be making carry legal, financial, and programmatic consequences that require human accountability.

Every skill in this collection is designed so that a professional can look at the output, verify the data, apply their judgment, and sign their name to the result. If a tool's output cannot be verified, or if the professional's judgment is not required to complete the work product, the tool has crossed the line.

These capabilities were evaluated and intentionally excluded. The tools that remain are better for it.
